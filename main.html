a tool to a medium, a medium to a tool.

Van de Seyp briefly mentioned something along the lines of how coding as a medium can achieve subtlety not achievable with other more traditional mediums. Which really gives rise to a thought that I have, do commonly used mediums change overtime with cultural and technological shifts?

One example I had was taking a look at paper as a medium. Like imagine a printed poster on a letter sized draft bond. Why is it so popular? Is it because the medium itself is extremely accessible, cheap to buy, and commonly found? Is it because the medium itself is versatile to accommodate print, digital work, folding, as well as different material performance in paper types and feels?

I wonder if coding, or the web-based work will ever develop into the same sort of versatility and stand on a similar pedestal as paper does today. In order for this to happen though, we're going to have to have:

  1. Even more versatility, could be in the form of AR, or incorporating more physical interfacing.
  2. Ease of access, tools could be developed in the future to make coding a lot more accessible for people, and advancements are already visible today.
  3. Dunno, just a thought on the potential progress of mediums from today.


ways of thinking

Another thing Van de Seyp mentioned during the lecture was her ways of thinking. She mentioned that she often starts more from curiosity, and embraces more unconventional ways of thinking. An example she gave was that she looked into how light refracted from a window, then built off of that unconventional concept. I gotta start incorporating this sort of exploration into my design process as well, as right now I think I find myself drifting towards more conventional design thinking, adhering to design paradigms, and mostly drawing inspiration from visual reference than abstract concepts.

I believe that taking a page out of her playbook and by drawing connotative inspiration from concepts, rather than the emulation of visual reference, we can probably create more “original” work. Not that I argue that any work is truly “original” nowadays, everyone has visual influences from works they digest, and nothing today is 100% original in the sense that it was not birthed from influence of another work. Which isn't a bad thing, it's amazing how the design and art space nurtures this method of thinking, and as a result I do not fret at the fact that none of my work is truly birthed from nothingness, created ex nihilo, but instead take pride knowing it is a cumulation of my experience and the works of others.

automation

Alas, we're back on AI again. It doesn't seem like we can escape the discourse around artificial intelligence today, especially given its exponential growth, and I guess we just gotta start integrating it into more discussions, and work today actually.

Van de Seyp showcased a pretty amazing automation tool for type design. I believe it will automatically generate glyphs given a small sample size of glyphs you make in order to get a rough feel for the aesthetic and theme of the typeface. Although not fully polished and incredibly groundbreaking it is still a pretty amazing tool, and it really got me thinking about a general theme that AI is taking design.

“AI should do my dishes so I can focus on actually creating creative work, not the other way around.” (believe this quote was taken from a conference Yigit attended, I saw in his instagram sometime ago)

I completely agree with this statement, and it got me thinking about to what extent, or rather, what can we consider as “mundane” work that should be automated? I think this is an incredibly nuanced area of discourse, especially considering the definition of mundane will drastically differ from person to person. I find typesetting to be ok, it's not too bad but if there existed a plugin that would be able to automatically typeset my documents and layouts, I would jump at that opportunity. Personally, I find the most enjoyment in creating layouts, compositions, and thinking more so about the big picture rather than getting granular with more technical details. Josh is different, that dude likes typesetting, but hey to each and their own, but that's what I'm saying, everyone has differentiating values regarding what they regard as mundane, so ultimately what is gonna get automated first if there is no general consensus on what is mundane labor?

With how tech is trending today, I think the focus on automation will literally just be whatever seems to be easier to achieve in the near future given the technological progress of today. Like if big tech thinks that automating typesetting will be achievable, and especially, economically viable and beneficial, they will focus on that.
